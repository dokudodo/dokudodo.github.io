How To Make Bar Soap At Home In Kenya A Step By Step Guide

If you’re tired of buying expensive bar soaps that are filled with harsh chemicals, making your own soap at home can be a great alternative. Not only is it cheaper, but you can also customize your soap to your liking with natural ingredients. In this guide, we’ll show you how to make bar soap at home in Kenya.

Materials Needed

Before you start making soap, you’ll need to gather the following materials:

  • 1 kg of olive oil
  • 500 g of coconut oil
  • 200 g of lye (sodium hydroxide)
  • 550 ml of distilled water
  • Essential oils or fragrance oils (optional)
  • Natural coloring (optional)
  • Soap molds
  • Safety gear (gloves, glasses, long sleeves)

Step 1: Prep Your Materials

Before you start making soap, it’s important to prepare your materials. First, put on your safety gear, including gloves, glasses, and long sleeves. Next, measure out your oils and set them aside. Then, weigh out your lye and water in separate containers.

Step 2: Melt the Oils

Next, melt your oils in a large pot over low heat. Once the oils are melted, remove the pot from the heat and let it cool to around 100-110°F.

Step 3: Mix the Lye Solution

While your oils are cooling, mix your lye and water together in a separate container. It’s important to add the lye to the water, not the other way around, as adding water to lye can cause a dangerous chemical reaction. Stir the lye and water together until the lye is completely dissolved.

Step 4: Combine the Oils and Lye Solution

Once your oils and lye solution are both around 100-110°F, slowly pour the lye solution into the pot with the oils while stirring constantly. Continue stirring until the mixture reaches trace, which is when the soap batter thickens to a pudding-like consistency. This can take anywhere from 5-30 minutes depending on how vigorously you stir and the temperature of your ingredients.

Step 5: Add Fragrance and Color

If you want to add fragrance and color to your soap, now is the time to do so. Add a few drops of essential oils or fragrance oils and stir well. If you want to add natural coloring, mix it in now as well.

Step 6: Pour Soap into Molds

Once your soap batter is ready, pour it into your soap molds. Tap the molds gently on a flat surface to release any air bubbles. Cover the molds with a towel or plastic wrap to keep them warm.

Step 7: Let Soap Harden

Let your soap harden for 24-48 hours. Once it’s firm to the touch, remove it from the molds and cut it into bars. Let the bars cure for 4-6 weeks before using them, as this allows the excess water to evaporate and the soap to harden.

Tips for Making Bar Soap

  • Always wear safety gear when working with lye.
  • Use a digital scale for precise measurements.
  • Make sure your work area is well ventilated.
  • Experiment with different oils and fragrances to find your perfect soap recipe.
  • Don’t rush the curing process, as it’s important for the soap’s quality.

FAQs

Is it safe to make bar soap at home?

Yes, as long as you take proper safety precautions, making soap at home is safe. Always wear gloves, glasses, and long sleeves, work in a well-ventilated area, and follow the recipe carefully.

Can I use different oils in my soap recipe?

Yes, you can experiment with different oils to create your perfect soap recipe. Just be sure to use a soap calculator to adjust the amount of lye needed based on the oils you’re using.

How long does it take for soap to cure?

Soap typically needs to cure for 4-6 weeks before it’s ready to use. This allows excess water to evaporate and the soap to harden.

Pros and Cons of Making Your Own Soap

Pros

  • Customizable: You can choose your own ingredients and customize your soap to your liking.
  • Cheaper: Making soap at home is often cheaper than buying it from a store.
  • Natural: You can use natural ingredients to make your soap, avoiding harsh chemicals found in many commercial soaps.

Cons

  • Safety: Working with lye can be dangerous if proper safety precautions aren’t taken.
  • Curing time: Soap needs to cure for several weeks before it’s ready to use, requiring patience.
  • Experimentation: It may take some experimentation to find the perfect soap recipe for you.
